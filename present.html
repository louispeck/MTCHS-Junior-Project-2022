Today, 3D printing is used in a wide array of different industries and applications. From hobby use to life saving medical treatments, 3D printing has opened the door to many different and rapidly advancing technologies. Three common applications of this technology are prototyping and manufacturing, medicine, and construction.

3D printing had first been developed as a means for faster prototyping. With a traditional molded prototype it might cost hundreds of thousands of dollars, while also taking weeks to produce a single mold. It is highly impractical if one is trying to improve on a design with each iteration. 3D printing technology reduces the lead times required in traditional manufacturing, which allows a prototype to be fabricated in mere hours rather than weeks at a fraction of the regular cost.

To start the 3D printing process a Computer Aided Design (CAD) file is generated after the design is completed and converted into a Standard Tessellation Language (STL) file. This conversion allows the 3D printer to create an object using geometric data. The four main parts are the UltraViolet (UV) curable photopolymer liquid, perforated table, laser source, and a computer to control the 3D printer. The table liquid polymer is then released onto the table where the UV laser then hardens the polymer which creates a layer. The machine then moves onto the next layer where it repeats the process until the item is finished. (Jandyal, Chaturvedi, Wazir, Raina & Haq, 2022)
